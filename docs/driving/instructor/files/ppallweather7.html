All Weather Driving
Skidding


Avoidance

-Skids are caused by braking, steering or accelerating too sharply for the road condition.

-Skids can be avoided by suiting your driving to the road condition. Slow down accordingly and use extra caution if the road surface is wet, snowy, icy, frosty, gravelly, muddy, leafy, oily.

-Even on good, dry roads, slow down accordingly where there is a greater chance of having to stop or steer suddenly ie potential hazards.

-Understand and maintain the appropriate separation distance behind vehicles.

-Concentrate fully on the road ahead at all times. Do not be distracted by phones, mirrors, food, passengers or anything else as you drive. Pull over where safe if tired.

-Make sure your tyres are in good condition (at least 1.6mm deep tread over the centre ¾) and that they are inflated to the appropriate pressures as indicated in your vehicle handbook.

Correction

-If you find yourself in a skid, make sure you’re off the accelerator and steer into it. If the rear of the vehicle is moving to the right then steer right. Steer as much or little as you need to straighten up. Oversteering can put the skid in the other direction.

-Know if your car has ABS (Anti Lock Braking System):

Cars without ABS: Deal with a skid by releasing the brake fully until control is regained. In a low speed skid you can pump the brake pedal to try and regain control.

Cars with ABS: Maintain pressure on the brake pedal. The brakes will automatically pump on and off very quickly to enable effective steering whilst in a skid. ABS won’t prevent you from skidding but will help you control the car in a skid. You will feel a juddering in the brake pedal when it is operating.

-Understand the difference between skidding and aquaplaning. See above.
